# 405 External Reference Notes

Reviewed on 2026-04-27. These sources support the local Phase 8 release-control contract; the local blueprint remains the source of truth when a source is broader or less strict.

## Official Sources Reviewed

| Source | Borrowed into 405 | Rejected or constrained |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [NHS England Digital IM1 Pairing integration](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/digital-services-for-integrated-care/im1-pairing-integration) | Borrowed the current IM1 posture that AI-containing products require whole-product documentation review, including DCB0129 safety material, DPIA, medical-device registration where applicable, and subprocessor safety evidence where needed. Also borrowed the RFC rule for material evolution from the assured use case, especially AI or significant functional enhancement. | Did not treat IM1 pairing as AI-specific technical assurance. The contract requires local replay, safety, supplier, and rollout proof. |
| [NHS England Digital SCAL process](https://digital.nhs.uk/developer/assurance/scal-process-for-apis-and-services) | Borrowed SCAL as a structured document-based assurance process spanning technical conformance, clinical safety, information governance and security, and organisational/business process risk. | Did not make SCAL completion a runtime safety signal. It is one evidence input to a pinned baseline and approval graph. |
| [NHS England Digital assurance overview](https://digital.nhs.uk/developer/assurance) | Borrowed the baseline that NHS assurance protects patients, data, and systems, and that DTAC is a national baseline for digital health tools. | Did not collapse local release control into a single NHS assurance artifact. 405 keeps release state, rollback, and freeze controls separate. |
| [NHS England guidance on AI-enabled ambient scribing products](https://www.england.nhs.uk/publication/guidance-on-the-use-of-ai-enabled-ambient-scribing-products/) | Borrowed the requirements for clinical safety officer involvement, DCB0160 documentation, DPIA, integration planning, user review and approval, staff training, output accuracy audits, incident review, and performance monitoring. | Did not allow pilots, shadow mode, or staff review to bypass candidate identity or approval topology. |
| [NHS Transformation Directorate DTAC guidance](https://transform.england.nhs.uk/key-tools-and-info/digital-technology-assessment-criteria-dtac/) | Borrowed the five DTAC areas: clinical safety, data protection, technical security, interoperability, and usability/accessibility; also borrowed that DTAC applies alongside, not instead of, medical-device, DSPT, DCB, and local governance. | Did not rely on the retiring Transformation Directorate site as the only source. The contract also references current NHS Digital assurance and AI and Digital Regulations Service material. |
| [AI and Digital Regulations Service DTAC developer guidance](https://digitalregulations.innovation.nhs.uk/regulations-and-guidance-for-developers/all-developers-guidance/using-the-digital-technology-assessment-criteria-dtac/) | Borrowed lifecycle evidence discipline and the same core DTAC areas, plus the link between intended purpose and medical-device status. | Did not make DTAC an approval substitute for IM1 RFC, DCB, DPIA, MHRA, release, or rollback proof. |
| [NHS England Digital clinical risk management standards](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/clinical-safety/clinical-risk-management-standards) | Borrowed the current manufacturer/deployment split: DCB0129 for manufacture of health IT systems and DCB0160 for deployment/use by health organisations, with mandatory status under the Health and Social Care Act 2012. | Did not decide every capability is a medical device. The contract requires assessment refs and deltas when boundaries or risk change. |
| [DCB0129/DCB0160 applicability step-by-step guidance](https://digital.nhs.uk/services/clinical-safety/applicability-of-dcb-0129-and-dcb-0160/step-by-step-guidance) | Borrowed intended-use, direct-care, medical-device, and implemented-in-health-IT boundary checks. This shaped the `medicalPurposeBoundaryState` and DCB trigger rules. | Did not allow a non-medical-device conclusion to bypass clinical risk management when the capability supports direct care. |
| [MHRA software and AI as a medical device guidance](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/software-and-artificial-intelligence-ai-as-a-medical-device/software-and-artificial-intelligence-ai-as-a-medical-device) | Borrowed intended-purpose assessment, SaMD/AIaMD lifecycle attention, post-market vigilance, incident reporting, and change-control sensitivity for software and AI. | Did not perform product classification in 405. The contract freezes machine-readable hooks and blockers for release and later safety-case work. |
| [AI and Digital Regulations Service guidance on improving or updating medical devices after deployment](https://digitalregulations.innovation.nhs.uk/regulations-and-guidance-for-developers/all-developers-guidance/improving-or-updating-medical-devices-after-deployment/) | Borrowed the need to assess significance of updates, reassess intended purpose, consider reapproval, maintain benefit-risk analysis, and respond to safety concerns. | Did not treat non-medical-device changes as ungoverned. The fail-closed matrix still routes material AI, supplier, data, and safety changes through release control. |

## Contract Implications

- IM1 RFC and SCAL update decisions must be explicit for material AI or functional change.
- DTAC deltas are evaluated alongside DCB, DPIA, IM1, and medical-device decisions.
- Medical-purpose boundary shifts require reassessment before promotion.
- Supplier and subprocessor assurance freshness is a promotion gate.
- Rollback readiness and freeze semantics are part of approval law, not operational afterthoughts.
