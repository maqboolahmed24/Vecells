## Core end-to-end product blueprint for Vecells

Vecells should be built as a **FHIR-native primary care access and operations layer**, not as a thin appointment-booking front end. That means FHIR-native at the clinical and partner boundary, but Vecells-first in its internal domain model. The core product normalises demand from **web, NHS App jump-off, and phone** into one safety-gated request pipeline, then routes each case to the right endpoint: self-care/info, admin resolution, clinician message or callback, local booking, PCN hub booking, or Pharmacy First, while keeping patients updated and staff working from a single operational view. That is the through-line of the layered architecture on pages 1 to 4 and the journey diagrams on pages 6 to 8 of your PDF.

That direction still fits the current NHS integration landscape. NHS England says **GP Connect Appointment Management new supplier development is paused**; **IM1 Pairing** remains the live route for integrating with principal GP systems and currently covers **EMIS Web and TPP SystmOne**, with **no current plans to decommission IM1**; **GP Connect Update Record** is limited to registered community pharmacy professionals using assured supplier and GP-system combinations; **NHS login** remains the patient authentication rail; and **MESH** remains a supported secure messaging rail for cross-organisation workflows. ([NHS England Digital][1])

### 1. Product definition

Vecells is best understood as a **demand orchestration system for primary care**. It is the front door for patients, the control tower for staff, and the coordination layer between practices, PCN hubs, pharmacies, and patient comms. The key architectural shift in your paper is the right one: **stop designing the product around federated booking through GP Connect**, and instead make the product rely on **IM1-first local automation** plus **coordination-first network booking** for Enhanced Access and cross-site workflows.

So the product should not think in terms of "appointments first." It should think in terms of **governed request lineages**. A patient begins in one `SubmissionEnvelope`; every ingress append first settles one immutable `SubmissionIngressRecord` under the published `IntakeConvergenceContract`; governed submit promotion then freezes one canonical `NormalizedSubmission` and creates a single canonical request item exactly once; safety logic runs; triage produces a structured outcome; workflow logic chooses the right endpoint; and only some cases become appointments. That is exactly what the page 6 to 8 flows show: intake first, endpoint decision second, booking only when clinically or operationally necessary.

Where booking is required, supplier differences in search, hold behavior, commit proof, and manage support must stay behind one published `BookingProviderAdapterBinding` compiled from the current `ProviderCapabilityMatrix` plus `AdapterContractProfile`. The booking core, queue logic, and patient or staff shells consume projected capability and settlement truth, not supplier-local semantics.

### 3. The canonical request model

The heart of the system should be a **single canonical request object**. Every governed submitted interaction, regardless of channel, becomes one request with a stable ID and a complete audit trail. Pre-submit capture should live in a `SubmissionEnvelope`, which holds draft evidence, continuation state, and partial identity context until governed promotion occurs, while each channel append first materializes one immutable `SubmissionIngressRecord` under the relevant `IntakeConvergenceContract`. At minimum the canonical request should carry patient identity status, source channel, request type, symptom or admin narrative, attachments or audio, contact preference, safety flags, triage result, priority band, assigned queue, chosen endpoint, SLA clocks, outbound communications, closure state, and the frozen normalized-submission or ingress references that explain how the request was formed. The page 6 diagram is explicit that both digital and telephony paths collapse into one governed request item before triage.

I would model that request as an internal domain object that is **represented clinically through FHIR resources**, not forced into one FHIR resource only. The cleanest pattern is: **Task** for the operational work item, **ServiceRequest** when there is an external or clinical service destination, **DocumentReference** for files, audio, and transcripts, **Consent** for clinically meaningful sharing controls, **Communication** for structured messaging, and **AuditEvent/Provenance** for traceability. The important discipline is that these are mapped representations, not the hidden write model: `AccessGrant`, `Session`, `RequestLifecycleLease`, booking or pharmacy cases, and other Vecells control objects remain internal aggregates even when they later emit FHIR companions. Your PDF already anchors Task, ServiceRequest, DocumentReference, and Consent as the core pattern, and that is the right spine to keep.

<!-- Architectural correction: drafts live on `SubmissionEnvelope`, not on `Request`, so web, phone, continuation links, and wrong-patient repair all share one pre-request staging model. -->
Do not overload one status field with identity, workflow, and safety. The platform should carry four explicit state axes across intake and request lifecycle:

- `submissionEnvelopeState`: `draft -> evidence_pending -> ready_to_promote -> promoted | abandoned | expired`
- `workflowState`: `submitted -> intake_normalized -> triage_ready -> triage_active -> handoff_active -> outcome_recorded -> closed`
- `safetyState`: `not_screened -> screen_clear | residual_risk_flagged | urgent_diversion_required -> urgent_diverted`
- `identityState`: `anonymous | partial_match | matched | claimed`

`submitted` is a real durable post-submit state and must be entered at the moment governed promotion from `SubmissionEnvelope` to `Request` succeeds. It is the anchor for replay, crash recovery, dedupe, and SLA timing. `intake_normalized` is also a real durable state and must only be entered after the immutable submission snapshot has been frozen and canonical normalization has succeeded. `triage_ready` is only legal once a real triage task exists.

That boundary needs one immutable bridge. `SubmissionPromotionRecord` is the only legal envelope-to-request mapping: it freezes the promoted `EvidenceSnapshot`, its `EvidenceCaptureBundle`, the authoritative `NormalizedSubmission`, current draft version, pinned `IntakeExperienceBundle`, receipt and status consistency keys, superseded draft grants or leases, and the handoff into `PatientJourneyLineage`. Receipt assembly, replay handling, silent re-auth, secure-link recovery, and embedded resume must resolve through this record rather than inferring promotion from route state, client cache, or a stale draft token.

The evidence contract itself also needs to be explicit. `EvidenceSnapshot` is not just a reassuring label over mutable payload, transcript, or summary state; it is one immutable source bundle plus pinned normalized and derived packages, redaction transforms, and one `EvidenceSummaryParityRecord` proving which visible summary still matches the frozen source. Late transcript improvement, enrichment, schema migration, or summary regeneration may append new derivatives, but if they change safety or patient-visible meaning they must mint a superseding snapshot instead of rewriting the old one.

The same applies to safety truth. Any inbound evidence batch that can affect urgency, reachability, or routine continuation must first settle one immutable `EvidenceAssimilationRecord`, one explicit `MaterialDeltaAssessment`, and one immutable `EvidenceClassificationDecision`, then, if preemption is required, one `SafetyPreemptionRecord` plus one `SafetyDecisionRecord` that advances the lineage `safetyDecisionEpoch`. `urgent_diversion_required` and `urgent_diverted` are separate durable states: the latter is legal only after one `UrgentDiversionSettlement` proves the urgent advice, escalation, or handoff was actually issued.

`triage_active` is the coarse canonical state for any live practice-side review on the same request lineage. Detailed operational states such as queued, claimed, in review, awaiting patient info, resumed review, escalated, reopened, and approval handling live on `TriageTask`, not on `Request` itself.

`residual_risk_flagged` means the request did not meet hard-stop urgent-diversion criteria, but one or more persisted residual-safety rules still apply and must influence queue priority and review behaviour. `urgent_diversion_required` means the safety engine has already determined that urgent handling is required, but the urgent advice, escalation, or callback action has not yet been durably issued. Once that action is durably issued, the safety axis must move to `urgent_diverted`.

Booking, hub, pharmacy, callback, messaging, and admin-resolution work should then live in dedicated child aggregates that reference the same request lineage rather than forcing the request itself into every operational substate. The canonical `Request` should carry milestone states such as active triage, active handoff, authoritative outcome, and final closure, while detailed child lifecycles stay on `BookingCase`, `HubCoordinationCase`, `PharmacyCase`, `CallbackCase`, `ClinicianMessageThread`, and `AdminResolutionCase` through one canonical `RequestLineage` plus `LineageCaseLink` contract.

At summary level, do not describe `Request` as if milestone state were sufficient by itself. The canonical request contract also needs authoritative control references such as `currentIdentityBindingRef`, `currentEvidenceSnapshotRef`, `requestLineageRef`, `currentTriageTaskRef`, `latestLineageCaseLinkRef`, `activeLineageCaseLinkRefs[]`, `currentConfirmationGateRefs[]`, and `currentClosureBlockerRefs[]`, with `patientRef` remaining nullable and derived from verified `IdentityBinding`. Only `LifecycleCoordinator` may derive cross-domain milestone changes and write `Request.workflowState = closed`; child services emit milestones, blocker changes, leases, and evidence through `LineageCaseLink`, but they do not write canonical closure or reconciliation meaning directly.

The same summary discipline now applies to rollout and trust posture. If a route is embedded or otherwise channel-bound, writable or bridge-backed access must also depend on the current `ReleaseApprovalFreeze`, `ChannelReleaseFreezeRecord`, route-scoped `PatientEmbeddedNavEligibility`, and negotiated `BridgeCapabilityMatrix`; if a booking, pharmacy, assistive, or release decision depends on an `AssuranceSliceTrustRecord` that is `degraded` or `quarantined`, this summary should be read as diagnostic-only continuity, not as permission to treat that slice as authoritative truth.

### 4. The end-to-end patient journey

High-priority journey gaps in this layer:

1. submission, receipt, status, and downstream endpoint views are described linearly, but not yet bound to one explicit request-lineage continuity contract
2. urgent diversion, degraded acceptance, and routine receipt are not separated sharply enough, which risks false reassurance or generic failure handling after the platform has already accepted patient input
3. receipt and ETA are not yet tied to one authoritative consistency envelope, so later status views could oscillate or contradict the originally issued promise
4. cancel, reschedule, update-details, more-info response, callback response, and message reply are named as persistent patient capabilities, but not yet governed by typed capability lease and route ownership
5. downstream endpoint transitions do not yet promote identity, reachability, consent, delivery, or reconciliation blockers as first-class in-line recovery states

The patient journey in the PDF is already strong and should become the canonical runtime behaviour, but it now needs a stricter shell and settlement contract. A patient arrives from web, NHS App, or phone, authenticates or is identified, submits a request, passes through a **red-flag safeguard gate**, and then stays inside the same request-lineage shell for every ordinary state change. Successful submission must morph the intake review surface into the receipt surface in place through `PersistentShell`, `CasePulse`, `StateBraid`, and the shared status strip, not a detached receipt page or blank reset.

Urgent diversion, degraded acceptance, and routine receipt must be different journey outcomes. If the safety gate determines urgent handling, the active step should be replaced in place with the urgent path. If the platform has already accepted the submission but normalization, downstream task creation, or authoritative projection assembly is degraded, the lineage must open `FallbackReviewCase`, issue `patient.receipt.degraded` or equivalent recovery guidance, and keep the patient in the same shell with a calm next-step explanation rather than dropping to a generic error. Only when submission, normalization, and task creation settle cleanly may the patient receive the standard receipt with status tracking.

Receipt, status, and later request-detail views must materialize under one `PatientShellConsistencyProjection`, the Phase 1 receipt contract, and one `PatientReceiptConsistencyEnvelope` frozen from the promoted `NormalizedSubmission`. The issued receipt bucket must be the smallest bucket whose calibrated on-time probability clears policy against the current upper-bound ETA, later status views may improve it only when fresher authoritative projections and hysteresis rules justify the change, and any downward revision must surface an explicit promise-change or recovery state rather than silently stretching the ETA. Browser, embedded, secure-link, and telephony-origin views may vary presentation or helpful provenance copy, but they may not fork ETA or status semantics for the same request lineage. No downstream child route may show final reassurance while booking, pharmacy, callback, or clinician-message work is still pending confirmation, disputed, or identity-held.

After triage, the page 7 outcome model still shows the correct product shape: a case can resolve into admin completion, self-care plus safety-netting, clinician message or callback, Pharmacy First referral, or appointment flow. The key product insight is that **appointment is one endpoint, not the default**. Self-care versus admin completion must now resolve through one `SelfCareBoundaryDecision` over `boundaryTupleHash`, with `SelfCareExperienceProjection` and `AdminResolutionExperienceProjection` proving whether the case is still informational, bounded admin-only, or reopened for clinician review. The same request or child shell should morph into `PatientIdentityHoldProjection`, `PatientReachabilitySummaryProjection`, `PatientConsentCheckpointProjection`, or `PatientActionSettlementProjection` as appropriate, preserving the original lineage anchor while making the next safe action explicit. Reachability blockers now have to point at the same current `ReachabilityAssessmentRecord` and `ContactRouteSnapshot` across patient, staff, support, booking, callback, and pharmacy surfaces, so stale verification, stale demographics, stale preferences, or disputed delivery cannot be mistaken for a healthy contact path in one surface but not another. Later views may refine or improve the promise only when a newer authoritative projection justifies the change; they may not silently downgrade, widen beyond the issued bucket, or contradict what has already been issued without an explicit recovery or dispute state.

**PatientManagedActionLease**
`actionLeaseId`, `actionType = cancel | reschedule | update_details | more_info_response | callback_response | message_reply`, `governingObjectRef`, `governingDecisionRef`, `entityVersionRef`, `policyBundleRef`, `routeOwner`, `expiresAt`

`PatientManagedActionLease` is the only legal path for persistent patient actions. If booking truth, message state, consent, route ownership, or policy changes underneath the action, the lease must fail closed and redirect into the correct in-shell review or repair state instead of mutating stale records.

**PatientRecoveryLoop**
`recoveryLoopId`, `journeyLineageRef`, `dominantBlocker`, `blockingProjectionRef`, `repairActionRef`, `fallbackChannelRef`, `resumeActionLeaseRef`

`PatientRecoveryLoop` turns identity holds, reachability failure, consent renewal, delivery dispute, pharmacy reconciliation, and callback repair into first-class patient journey states. Only one recovery loop may dominate the lineage at a time, and it must reopen the same `ContactRouteRepairJourney` or other bound blocker path with the preserved anchor and continuation, then resume the interrupted action through a fresh lease rather than sending the patient into a generic support dead end.

### 5. Clinical workspace and operational workflow

The page 8 staff flow is the right operating model for practices. Staff open a work queue, review the triage summary and AI suggestions, ask for more information if needed, choose the endpoint, and either resolve directly, escalate for risk, route to Pharmacy First, or hand off to booking. The most important part of that flow is the **human checkpoint before irreversible clinical action**. The system can suggest, summarise, rank, and prepare documentation, but signing, confirming, coding, or clinically irreversible action must remain human-approved.

That operating model should be backed by a prioritisation engine that combines **clinical priority, time-to-breach, queue aging, vulnerability/equity markers, continuity preferences, and practice-configured routing rules**. Your PDF mentions duplicate suppression, same-episode review, fairness floors, and queue controls; those should be implemented as core platform behaviour, not as dashboard analytics. Suspected duplicates must create governed `DuplicateCluster` work, exact and semantic replays must collapse only through canonical `IdempotencyRecord` plus authoritative settlement proof, identifier reuse with divergent payload must open `ReplayCollisionReview`, and `same_episode_candidate` cases must never silently auto-merge. Same-request attach now also requires immutable `DuplicatePairEvidence`, append-only `DuplicateResolutionDecision`, explicit continuity witness, and candidate-competition proof so one plausible target request cannot silently absorb another. Canonical queue order must come from one versioned `QueueRankPlan` and one replayable `QueueRankSnapshot`, while reviewer-fit and assignment suggestions stay downstream and may not rewrite that shared order. In other words, Vecells does not merely show the queue; it actively **shapes the queue**.

That same queue and workspace layer must stay subordinate to the current safety epoch. Late patient replies, callback outcomes, booking-manage symptom changes, pharmacy bounce-backs, or degraded evidence cannot just flip a badge and carry on; they must append new `EvidenceClassificationDecision` and `SafetyDecisionRecord` rows, raise `WorkspaceSafetyInterruptProjection` or the equivalent same-shell safety surface, and suppress calm completion or next-task progression until the current epoch is settled.

The **Operations Console** should therefore be powered by denormalised read models built from the event stream: live queue state, upcoming SLA breaches, volume by pathway, waitlist conversion, pharmacy bounce-backs, unresolved high-risk items, and access inequity signals by channel and cohort. That is exactly consistent with the "analytics and assurance" plus "operations console" definition in the PDF.

### 6. Booking and access continuity

Booking should be implemented as **two coordinated capabilities**. First is the **Local Booking Orchestrator**, which uses IM1 where supplier and permission scope allow creation, update, and synchronisation of booking artefacts inside GP systems. Second is the **Network Coordination Desk**, which handles cases that need cross-site or hub coordination with structured tasks, ranked options, timers, and patient updates. That split is already described in your architecture and the page 7/8 flows show how it should behave in practice.

Official IM1 guidance supports this direction. IM1 standards are active, currently pair to EMIS Web and TPP SystmOne, and the patient-facing/API capabilities include appointment view, booking, amend/cancel, repeat medication, patient communications, record access, and transaction-level filing of data and documents, subject to pairing and supplier permissions. Because supplier functionality varies and detailed interface packs arrive after feasibility/pairing, Vecells should hide every GP-system interaction behind a **supplier capability matrix and adapter interface**, not hard-code business logic to one vendor's API behaviour. ([NHS England Digital][3])

For Enhanced Access, the page 8 hub desk flow should become a dedicated coordination workflow: open request, evaluate window and needs, book in the hub's native system if suitable, otherwise solve one diverse open-choice alternative set with a separate callback fallback card, or escalate back to the duty clinician if urgency outruns capacity. NHS England's published Enhanced Access expectation remains weekday **6:30pm to 8pm** and Saturday **9am to 5pm**, so those windows should be treated as policy-configurable defaults inside the hub coordination rules. That policy should compile as one typed tuple over routing, approved variance windows, service obligations, practice-visibility obligations, and capacity-ingestion rules so patient offerability, acknowledgement debt, and ops exceptions all resolve from the same frozen pack set rather than local heuristics. ([NHS England][4])

The **Smart Waitlist** should be a real transactional subsystem, not a nice-to-have add-on. It should support opt-in state, ranked candidate generation, offer expiry, confirmation windows, anti-duplicate rules, and local or hub-level auto-fill. In product terms, booking becomes a controlled workflow of **offer, provisional hold, confirm, commit, notify**, with a fallback to hub desk or callback when commitment fails. The visible order and reason cues for those local and hub offers should come from one persisted `CapacityRankProof` plus candidate `CapacityRankExplanation` rows and audience-safe `CapacityRankDisclosurePolicy`, so patient `soonest` or `best match` copy, hub-coordinator explanation, support replay, and operations diagnostics all describe the same ordering decision.

Keep that summary subordinate to the canonical booking control plane. User-visible exclusivity must come from `ReservationAuthority` plus real `CapacityReservation`, not from countdown copy alone, and ambiguous supplier outcomes must materialize as `ExternalConfirmationGate` rather than optimistic booked-state reassurance. Local booking and hub coordination may emit milestones and evidence, but `LifecycleCoordinator` remains the only authority that translates those facts into canonical request closure or settled patient reassurance.

### 7. Pharmacy First pathway

The Pharmacy First part of Vecells should work as a **structured referral and closure loop**, not as direct GP record mutation by Vecells. Your PDF is correct that the product should enforce pathway eligibility, red-flag checks, and age/sex gating, create a referral pack for the patient and pharmacy, dispatch it through an approved operational route, and then capture the outcome back into GP workflow without trying to impersonate a community pharmacy system.

That remains aligned with the current NHS position. NHS England still describes Pharmacy First as including the **7 clinical pathways**, and GP Connect Update Record is still only for registered community pharmacy professionals using assured pharmacy and GP-system combinations. Update Record is for structured consultation summaries; it is **not** for urgent actions or referrals back to general practice, which must still use local processes such as NHSmail or telephone. So Vecells' core design should be: **eligibility engine + referral composer + pharmacy discovery + dispatch + outcome reconciliation**, not direct Update Record submission. ([NHS England][5])

For service discovery, I would now make **Directory of Healthcare Services (Service Search)** the main lookup rail for pharmacy and service routing, because NHS England describes it as the **strategic API** for service lookup and is guiding legacy dispenser lookup use cases toward it. In other words, the product's core should not depend on older, fragmented dispenser-search patterns when the strategic direction is already clearer. ([NHS England Digital][6])

Operationally, the Pharmacy First module should support patient choice, persisted pharmacy-choice proof with explicit recommended and warned explanations, opening-hours checks, referral expiry, pharmacy bounce-back handling, and outcome ingestion. Recommendations should stay advisory rather than funnelling, and a bounce-back should reopen the original case with increased visibility in the clinical queue, exactly as the page 8 staff flow suggests. The architecture should now make that loop two-stage on purpose: one discovery abstraction that freezes directory source snapshots and provider-capability snapshots into the choice tuple, then one transport abstraction that freezes a transport-neutral referral package before resolving a transport-bound dispatch plan, assurance profile, adapter binding, and proof chain. Directory deprecation, source failover, or transport fallback may supersede those tuples, but they may not silently reorder patient choice or reinterpret the frozen package.

Do not summarise pharmacy as if referral dispatch and closure were fire-and-forget. Referral consent must stay bound to provider, pathway, scope, choice proof, warned-choice acknowledgement when present, package lineage, and one authoritative checkpoint, dispatch must carry acknowledgement, retry, expiry, and correlation proof, and weakly correlated or email-only returns must not auto-close the originating request. If contact-route failure, consent expiry, consent supersession, post-dispatch revocation, transport ambiguity, or pharmacy-choice proof drift blocks the pathway, the clinical or patient surface must pivot to that blocker explicitly instead of implying a settled pharmacy outcome.

### 8. Data and platform architecture

The platform layer in your PDF is strong and should remain intact: a governed domain transaction store plus versioned FHIR representation storage for clinical truth, **Event Bus** for orchestration, **Object Store** for binary artefacts, **Feature Store** for longitudinal model features, and a **WORM Audit Ledger** for tamper-evident audit. That combination gives you one place for canonical Vecells lifecycle truth, one place for clinically interoperable representations, one place for async process coordination, one place for large artefacts, one place for machine features, and one place for immutable traceability.

In development terms, the cleanest implementation is a **CQRS-style architecture**. The write side commits clinical and operational changes into the Vecells domain model, materializes governed FHIR representation sets from published mapping contracts, and publishes domain events through an outbox. The read side builds fast projections for the patient status tracker, staff queues, hub desk board, waitlist engine, and analytics dashboards. That lets the GP-system integration layer stay slow and variable while the product UX remains responsive without forcing internal lifecycle truth to collapse into raw FHIR resources.

The event taxonomy must be one explicit cross-phase contract, not a phase-local convention. Adopt these namespaces as canonical across the whole blueprint: `request.*`, `intake.*`, `identity.*`, `access.*`, `telephony.*`, `safety.*`, `triage.*`, `booking.*`, `hub.*`, `pharmacy.*`, `patient.*`, `communication.*`, `reachability.*`, `exception.*`, `confirmation.*`, `capacity.*`, `support.*`, `assistive.*`, `policy.*`, `release.*`, `analytics.*`, and `audit.*`.

Use them consistently. For example: `request.created`, `request.submitted`, `request.workflow.changed`, `intake.draft.created`, `intake.normalized`, `identity.session.established`, `access.grant.issued`, `telephony.call.started`, `safety.screened`, `triage.task.created`, `booking.commit.confirmed`, `hub.offer.created`, `pharmacy.outcome.received`, `patient.receipt.issued`, `communication.queued`, `reachability.assessment.settled`, `confirmation.gate.created`, `policy.bundle.promoted`, `release.wave.started`, and `audit.break_glass.used`.

Do not let `ingest.*`, `tasks.*`, `fallback.review_case.*`, or `external.confirmation.gate.*` survive as parallel canonical namespaces. If a legacy producer or adapter still emits those names, map them into the published canonical event contracts before the events reach projections, analytics, or assurance.

### 9. AI and automation

The AI layer in Vecells should be **assistive, bounded, and auditable**. Its job is to perform intent classification, acuity scoring, red-flag extraction, complexity scoring, summarisation, suggested endpointing, transcript drafting, and documentation prefill. Its output should always resolve into a structured operational artefact: priority band, risk markers, proposed queue, and a reviewable recommendation bundle that may later materialize as mapped FHIR `Task` and `ServiceRequest` artifacts after human approval. That is directly aligned with the "clinical intelligence" and "documentation automation" services in the paper.

Two control rules should be non-negotiable. First, **no irreversible clinical action without human approval**; that is exactly what page 8 encodes. Second, every model output must be **versioned, attributable, replayable, and overrideable**. Store the model version, prompt/template version where relevant, input snapshot, output, confidence bands, and the human decision that accepted, amended, or rejected it.

Current NHS guidance around IM1 is relevant here. NHS England says the IM1 onboarding process will review whole-product documentation for AI-containing products, including the **DCB0129 clinical safety case, hazard log, DPIA, and related documentation**, but also says IM1 pairing does **not** provide AI-specific technical assurance of the models or algorithms themselves. So Vecells must carry its own model governance: offline evaluation, thresholding, shadow mode where needed, drift monitoring, clinician feedback capture, rollback, and clear fallback-to-manual behaviour. ([NHS England Digital][7])

### 10. Identity, consent, security, and policy

For patients, the identity rail should be **NHS login**. But the architecture has to respect what NHS login is and is not. NHS England states that NHS login allows patients to access services and handle identity verification, but it **does not provide clinical authorisation**, and **session management/logout remain partner responsibilities**. So patient authentication, application session management, consent capture, contact preference management, and feature authorisation must remain explicit Vecells responsibilities. ([NHS England Digital][8])

High-priority trust and policy gaps in this layer:

1. patient authentication is named, but there is no single authority record separating verified identity from live clinical or operational authorization
2. consent is mentioned broadly, but not as a provider-, pathway-, channel-, and object-bound grant with expiry, withdrawal, and post-action revocation behavior
3. session management is assigned to Vecells, but not yet bound to one authoritative session contract covering rotation, downgrade, revocation, embedded context, and recovery
4. staff RBAC plus ABAC is named, but break-glass and purpose-of-use elevation are not yet carried by one governed token with expiry, approver burden, and review trace
5. policy enforcement is central in prose, but there is no single decision witness proving which bundle, reason codes, and evidence set actually authorized or denied a given read or write

Carry this layer through seven cross-cutting control contracts:

**IdentityAuthorityRecord**
`identityAuthorityId`, `subjectRef`, `authSource`, `verifiedIdentityState`, `identityBindingRef`, `bindingVersionRef`, `ownershipState`, `assuranceBand`, `ageGateState`, `restrictionReasonRefs`, `authorizationCeilingRef`, `evaluatedAt`

**ConsentScopeGrant**
`consentScopeGrantId`, `subjectRef`, `scopeType = communication | referral | booking | record_visibility | embedded_channel`, `providerOrRouteRef`, `pathwayOrObjectRef`, `channelRef`, `grantedAt`, `expiresAt`, `revokedAt`, `postActionRevocationState`, `evidenceRef`

**SessionAuthorityRecord**
`sessionAuthorityId`, `subjectRef`, `identityBindingRef`, `sessionEpoch`, `subjectBindingVersionRef`, `channelContextRef`, `assuranceBand`, `sessionState = active | downgraded | reauth_required | revoked | expired`, `issuedAt`, `idleExpiresAt`, `absoluteExpiresAt`, `terminationReasonRef`

**AuthTransactionRecord**
`authTransactionId`, `scopeBundleRef`, `returnIntentRef`, `transactionFenceRef`, `transactionState`, `startedAt`, `completedAt`

**SessionEstablishmentRecord**
`sessionEstablishmentId`, `authTransactionRef`, `sessionAuthorityRef`, `identityBindingRef`, `subjectComparisonState`, `returnIntentDisposition`, `authorityState = auth_read_only | claim_pending | writable | recovery_only`, `decision`, `decidedAt`

**PurposeElevationToken**
`purposeElevationTokenId`, `actorRef`, `roleScopeRef`, `purposeOfUseRef`, `breakGlassState`, `approvedByRef`, `issuedAt`, `expiresAt`, `reviewRequiredAt`, `tokenState = active | expired | revoked | superseded`

**PolicyDecisionWitness**
`policyDecisionWitnessId`, `decisionType = read | write | elevation | consent | release`, `governingObjectRef`, `compiledPolicyBundleRef`, `reasonCodeRefs`, `inputEvidenceRefs`, `visibilityDecisionRef`, `actionScopeRef`, `decisionOutcome`, `recordedAt`

Make the operating rules explicit:

- NHS login may prove who the patient is, but every patient-visible or mutating capability must still derive from `IdentityAuthorityRecord`, current `SessionAuthorityRecord`, and any required `ConsentScopeGrant`
- patient binding may advance only through append-only `IdentityBinding` versions settled by `IdentityBindingAuthority`; auth callback, telephony verification, secure-link redemption, support correction, import, and backfill may contribute evidence or recovery intent only
- NHS login callback success may not itself imply request claim, writable resume, or PHI-bearing recovery. One frozen `AuthTransactionRecord` and one `SessionEstablishmentRecord` must first decide whether Vecells creates, rotates, reuses, or denies the local session, and whether the returned shell is only `auth_read_only`, `claim_pending`, `writable`, or bounded recovery
- consent may not be inferred from authentication, prior contact, or selected route alone; new provider, new pathway, new channel, or post-dispatch change must re-evaluate the applicable `ConsentScopeGrant`
- every sign-in, secure-link redemption, embedded NHS App return, timeout, logout, downgrade, or subject switch must rotate or settle the current `SessionAuthorityRecord`; stale session lineage must fail closed into same-shell recovery
- break-glass and just-in-time elevation must issue `PurposeElevationToken`, force reason-coded audit capture, surface review burden automatically, and expire back to ordinary scope without relying on manual cleanup
- every allow, deny, redact, suppress, or elevate decision must leave a `PolicyDecisionWitness` tied to the active compiled bundle and evidence set so assurance, support, and governance can replay why the system behaved as it did

For staff, use organisation-controlled SSO and enforce **RBAC plus ABAC** inside Vecells. The key policy controls from your PDF should remain central: **purpose-of-use checks, FHIR Consent enforcement, break-glass access, and immutable audit capture**. Break-glass should require a reason code, force heightened audit visibility, and be included automatically in assurance packs and governance review workflows.

If the patient experience is surfaced inside the NHS App, build the web portal to **NHS service standard and WCAG 2.2 AA** from day one. NHS England's current guidance makes those requirements explicit for NHS App integrations. That means accessibility, content design, error handling, and assisted-digital behaviour are not polishing tasks; they are core product architecture. ([NHS England Digital][2])

### 11. Analytics and assurance

The analytics layer should do two jobs simultaneously. First, it should run the service in real time: live volumes, queue health, demand by request type, triage distribution, booking outcomes, hub utilisation, waitlist fill, pharmacy referral outcomes, red-flag incidents, and patient comms status. Second, it should produce **assurance artefacts**: monthly packs for safety, access, equity, and operational performance, which your PDF explicitly calls out.

The most useful product stance is to treat analytics as an **operational subsystem**, not a BI afterthought. Queue routing, waitlist filling, fairness floors, and capacity shaping all need current state plus event history. So the analytics service should own both real-time projections and scheduled assurance outputs, with drill-down links back to the underlying request and audit evidence.

### 12. Practical engineering shape

A practical engineering shape for Vecells is a **modular platform with clear bounded contexts**, not a pile of tightly coupled microservices and not a single unstructured monolith. Keep the logical domains separate even if some are deployed together: intake/safety, triage intelligence, workflow/prioritisation, booking, pharmacy, documentation, patient comms, analytics, and audit. The GP-system adapters, MESH adapter, telephony adapter, and messaging adapter should be isolated behind stable internal contracts because those are the parts most exposed to variability and external change. That fits both the paper's layered model and the current reality that IM1 details vary by supplier and pairing pack. ([NHS England Digital][9])

Infrastructure-wise, the product should run on **UK-hosted cloud infrastructure**, keep binary artefacts in object storage, keep transactional clinical data in a FHIR-capable relational store, use an event bus plus outbox for reliable publishing, use a workflow/timer engine for long-running waits and escalations, and maintain an append-only audit store that cannot be silently rewritten. For developer documentation and conformance assets, do not anchor internal runbooks to the old `developer.nhs.uk` and legacy FHIR servers; NHS England decommissioned those on **2 March 2026** and points teams to maintained content on Digital pages, GitHub Pages, and Simplifier-hosted conformance assets. ([NHS England Digital][10])

A critical missing piece is the production runtime and release contract. The lifecycle, safety, and workflow logic are already unusually mature, but the blueprint still needs one cross-cutting source of truth for trust-zone topology, browser-to-gateway boundaries, environment rings, zero-downtime schema change rules, artifact provenance, and canary or rollback evidence. Without that, a correct domain model can still ship as an operationally brittle system. Add `platform-runtime-and-release-blueprint.md` as a first-class delivery artifact rather than treating deployment and release engineering as leftover DevOps detail.

### 13. The finished core product, in one line

When this core blueprint is implemented correctly, Vecells becomes **one access layer where every patient request enters once, is safety-screened once, becomes one canonical work object, is resolved through the correct endpoint, is reflected back into practice workflows through approved NHS rails, and is fully auditable end to end**. That is exactly what your layered architecture plus the page 6, 7, and 8 flows are pointing toward.

### 14. Programme Baseline With NHS App Deferred

NHS App integration can be deferred as a channel-expansion phase without breaking the current end-to-end plan. The near-term completion line should be: Phases 0 through 6 complete, Phases 8 and 9 complete, and the twelve cross-cutting front-end, lifecycle, and runtime blueprints above implemented as first-class programme deliverables. In that baseline, callback/message loops and self-care/admin-resolution loops must be treated as explicit domains, not thin endpoint labels.
